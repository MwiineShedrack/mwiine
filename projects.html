01

Security and Resilience in Data Systems

This paper argues that while frameworks like Kenya’s Data Protection Act and the Malabo Convention have strengthened privacy and compliance in Africa’s digital economy, they are not sufficient to ensure data system continuity during disruptions such as cyberattacks or infrastructure failures. It introduces a Resilience by Design paradigm that embeds resilience into the architecture, operations, and governance of data systems. The study integrates technical approaches such as zero trust architecture, fault tolerance, multi cloud redundancy, and immutable data storage, linking them to measurable resilience metrics like recovery time and data integrity loss. It also proposes governance reforms including resilience audits, stronger Computer Emergency Response Teams, and cross border coordination. The paper concludes with a tiered adoption roadmap and policy recommendations to harmonize resilience within existing data protection regimes, positioning resilient data systems as essential to Africa’s trusted and inclusive digital transformation..

Carbon Sequestration Strategies: Biochar as a Credit Engine in East Africa

Biochar production via pyrolysis is emerging in Kenya as a waste-to-value pathway that can simultaneously address organic waste burdens, soil fertility decline, and the growing market for durable carbon removals. This analysis synthesizes (i) interview-derived operational evidence from industrial producers and (ii) deskbased evidence spanning Kenya’s feedstock landscape, processing yields, standards/certification requirements, market pricing, carbon regulation, and emerging commercial models. Findings show that: (1) feedstock logistics and moisture variability, rather than absolute biomass availability, are the dominant constraints on unit economics; (2) carbon finance is frequently decisive for commercial viability at scale, but is gated by measurement, reporting and verification (MRV) and certification readiness; (3) Kenya’s regulatory environment increasingly requires international-grade certification and validation for carbon market participation through the Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations, 2024; and (4) scalable adoption in agriculture depends on embedding biochar into existing fertilizer/soil-input distribution channels, not treating it as a standalone niche product. Recommendations focus on (a) county-backed feedstock aggregation hubs, (b) demand anchoring through fertilizer firms and structured farmer programs, and (c) accelerated national MRV capability-building and standard alignment.

Macroeconomic Factors Affecting Exchange Rate Volatility in Kenya

This study examines exchange rate dynamics in Kenya, focusing on the Kenyan Shilling against major currencies including the US Dollar, Euro, Ugandan Shilling, and British Pound. While the KES/USD pair has historically drawn the most attention due to the dollar’s dominance in global trade and finance, the research expands the analysis to provide a broader and more holistic understanding of Kenya’s currency movements. The paper explores how macroeconomic factors such as inflation differentials, interest rate disparities, trade balances, remittance flows, and external shocks interact to shape exchange rate volatility in a small open economy. It highlights the significant depreciation and volatility of the Kenyan Shilling over the past two decades and evaluates the economic consequences for growth, price stability, trade competitiveness, and foreign denominated debt. Using advanced econometric techniques, including GARCH family models and vector autoregression, the study identifies key drivers of currency volatility and provides data driven insights relevant to monetary policy formulation and hedging strategies. By linking macroeconomic variables to exchange rate behavior, the research contributes practical and policy relevant understanding to economists, financial analysts, and policymakers.

07

2025 Society of Actuaries Research Case Study Challenge

Earthen dams are critical to Tarrodan’s (hypothetical country used for the research challenge) water supply, irrigation, hydropower, and flood control, yet their structural integrity is increasingly threatened by erosion, seismic activity, and climate change. This study develops and evaluates the National Earthen Dam Insurance Program (NEDIP), a comprehensive risk-transfer and resilience framework designed to safeguard fiscal stability while incentivizing infrastructure improvements. Leveraging the National Dam Risk Database (NDRD), actuarial modeling, and AI-powered underwriting, NEDIP introduces risk-based premium structures, semi-mandatory participation, and tiered subsidies to balance affordability with solvency. Financial projections from 2024–2045 demonstrate positive net cash flows across regions, supported by reinsurance treaties, government contributions, and adaptive reserve management. Sensitivity analyses confirm resilience under climate stress scenarios, while dynamic recalibration ensures premiums reflect evolving hazard profiles. Beyond insurance, NEDIP channels revenues into preventative maintenance and structural reinforcements, reducing long-term liabilities and enhancing dam safety. By integrating actuarial rigor, climate risk analytics, and infrastructure investment, NEDIP establishes a scalable model for national disaster risk financing, aligning fiscal sustainability with proactive resilience in the face of escalating environmental uncertainty.

National Insurance Program Financial Model

This financial model is built to assess the sustainability and risk-adjusted performance of a national insurance program covering dam-related hazards. It integrates actuarial calculations of expected losses, premium income, reinsurance costs, and catastrophe load factors, while adjusting for climate change impacts like floods, avalanches, and coastal erosion. The model accounts for risk scores tied to dam hazard, inspection delays, age, housing value, and infrastructure damage, then translates these into premiums, claims, recoveries, and capital reserve requirements. It also projects net cash flows over time, incorporating government subsidies, reinsurance recoveries, investment returns, and inflation adjustments, providing a comprehensive framework for evaluating long-term financial viability and resilience of the insurance program.

Climate · Investment

Biochar & Carbon Credits Facility Financial Model

This financial model is designed to evaluate the investment returns of a biochar and carbon credits facility in Kenya over a 10-year horizon. It allows users to input assumptions on capacity, utilization, feedstock costs, biochar pricing, carbon credit revenues, financing structure, and operating expenses, calculates production volumes, revenues, costs, depreciation, taxes, and financing schedules. The model outputs key investor metrics such as project IRR, NPV, equity IRR, payback period, and sensitivity analyses under different scenarios.
